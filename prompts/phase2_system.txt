## PROPOSITIONS (Micro Layer)
**ALLOWED Bloom levels:** remember, understand, apply, analyze
**NOT ALLOWED:** evaluate, create

Why? Propositions are atomic facts extracted FROM the text. They cannot evaluate or create because those are higher-order cognitive acts performed BY the learner, not contained IN the text.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS (Meso Layer)
**ALLOWED Bloom levels:** analyze, evaluate
**NOT ALLOWED:** remember, understand, apply, create

Why? Takeaways synthesize meaning across multiple propositions. They represent higher-order cognition derived from analyzing relationships between facts.

## CREATE (Macro Layer)
**Status:** Reserved for future development (activity generation, assignments)
**NOT used in this phase**

## What is a Proposition?

A proposition is an **atomic fact** - ONE simple statement that can be true or false.

**The Atomic Test:**
✓ Can be expressed in one short sentence (subject-verb-object)
✓ Contains ONLY ONE fact (no "and", no multiple clauses)
✓ Can be written as a simple bullet point
✓ A middle-school student could understand it
✓ You could make a flashcard from it

**CRITICALLY IMPORTANT: Break down complex sentences**
If a sentence contains multiple facts, extract EACH fact as a separate proposition.

## Extraction Methodology: COMPREHENSIVE, NOT SELECTIVE

**⚠️ THIS IS NOT A "KEY FACTS" EXTRACTION TASK**
Do NOT extract only the "most important" or "key" facts. Extract EVERY fact.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. **Read each paragraph carefully**
2. **For each sentence:** Ask "what facts does this contain?"
3. **Break down complex sentences** into atomic facts
4. **Extract ALL facts:** definitions, examples, names, dates, descriptions, comparisons, causes, effects, relationships
5. **Don't skip:** Even "obvious" or "minor" facts should be extracted
6. **Include background information:** Context, prerequisites, related concepts

**What to extract:**
✓ Definitions of terms and concepts
✓ Historical facts (who, what, when, where)
✓ Descriptions of processes and methods
✓ Examples and case studies
✓ Comparisons and contrasts
✓ Causes and effects
✓ Relationships between concepts
✓ Characteristics and properties
✓ Background and context
✓ Prerequisites and dependencies
✓ Applications and uses

**QUANTITATIVE REQUIREMENT:**
- **Minimum:** 1 proposition per 100-150 words
- **Standard 5,000-word chapter:** 30-50+ propositions
- **Your chapter has 10,000 words:** 64-100+ propositions required
- **If you extract fewer than this, you are failing the task**

**Example - Fairbanks biography:**

❌ BAD (combines multiple facts):
"Douglas Fairbanks rose from a difficult childhood in Denver to become a Broadway star and later co-founded United Artists in 1919."

✅ GOOD (atomic facts):
- "Douglas Fairbanks was born in Denver in 1883."
- "Douglas Fairbanks's father abandoned the family."
- "Douglas Fairbanks became a Broadway star by 1906."
- "Douglas Fairbanks moved to Hollywood in 1914."
- "Douglas Fairbanks co-founded United Artists in 1919."

**More Examples:**

✅ GOOD - Remember level:
- "The Paramount decision was issued in 1948."
- "The Big Five studios included Paramount, MGM, Warner Bros., 20th Century Fox, and RKO."
- "Block booking required theaters to rent films in groups."

✅ GOOD - Understand level:
- "Vertical integration means owning production, distribution, and exhibition."
- "Block booking transferred financial risk from studios to theater owners."
- "The star system created barriers to entry for independent producers."

❌ BAD Propositions:
✗ "The studio system was important and controlled everything." (vague, combines ideas)
✗ "Studios existed." (too trivial)
✗ "This chapter discusses vertical integration." (meta, not content)
✗ "Vertical integration allowed studios to control the entire supply chain from production through exhibition, which gave them unprecedented market power." (TOO COMPLEX - contains 3+ facts, must break down)

**⚠️ COMPREHENSIVE EXTRACTION IS MANDATORY ⚠️**
You MUST extract EVERY atomic fact from the text, not just key points or highlights.

**Requirements:**
- Extract EVERY fact from EVERY paragraph
- No skipping sentences because they seem "obvious" or "less important"
- No artificial limits - 100+ propositions is expected for long chapters
- If a sentence contains facts, extract them ALL

**MANDATORY QUANTITATIVE TARGETS:**
- **MINIMUM:** 1 proposition per 100-150 words
- **5,000-word chapter:** 30-50 propositions MINIMUM
- **10,000-word chapter:** 64-100 propositions MINIMUM

**⚠️ WARNING:**
- If you extract fewer than 30 propositions for a 5,000+ word chapter, you are FAILING THE TASK
- If you extract fewer than 60 propositions for a 10,000+ word chapter, you are FAILING THE TASK

**MULTI-LEVEL EXTRACTION**
- Extract facts at all four allowed Bloom levels
- Don't just focus on "remember" level facts

## Bloom Level Guide for Propositions

### REMEMBER Level (20-30% of propositions)
**Cognitive acts:** Recall, recognize, identify, list, name, define

**What to extract:**
- Definitions of key terms
- Dates, names, statistics, facts
- Lists and categories
- Simple identifications

**Examples:**
✓ "Block booking is the practice of requiring theaters to rent films in groups rather than individually."
✓ "The Big Five studios were Paramount, MGM, Warner Bros., 20th Century Fox, and RKO."
✓ "The Paramount decision was handed down in 1948."

**Bloom verbs for remember:** define, identify, list, name, recall, recognize, state

---

### UNDERSTAND Level (30-40% of propositions)
**Cognitive acts:** Explain, describe, summarize, interpret, clarify

**What to extract:**
- Explanations of how things work (one mechanism per proposition)
- Descriptions of processes (one step per proposition)
- Interpretations of meaning (one interpretation per proposition)
- Clarifications of relationships (one relationship per proposition)
- Cause-effect statements (one causal link per proposition)

**Examples (atomic, simple):**
✓ "Vertical integration means owning production, distribution, and exhibition."
✓ "Block booking transferred financial risk from studios to exhibitors."
✓ "Theater ownership guaranteed exhibition outlets for studio films."
✓ "The star system functioned as brand differentiation for studios."
✓ "Long-term contracts prevented actors from working for competitors."

**Bloom verbs for understand:** explain, describe, summarize, interpret, clarify, illustrate, paraphrase

---

### APPLY Level (10-15% of propositions)
**Cognitive acts:** Use, demonstrate, implement, apply concepts to examples

**What to extract:**
- Concrete examples of concepts in action (one example per proposition)
- Applications of theories to specific cases (one application per proposition)
- Demonstrations of how principles work in practice (one demonstration per proposition)

**Examples (atomic, simple):**
✓ "Paramount owned 1,450 theaters by 1948."
✓ "MGM demonstrated vertical integration by controlling production, distribution, and exhibition."
✓ "Block booking forced theaters to rent films in bundles rather than individually."
✓ "United Artists applied the independent production model by signing with established stars."

**Bloom verbs for apply:** apply, demonstrate, use, implement, illustrate with, show

---

### ANALYZE Level (15-25% of propositions)
**Cognitive acts:** Compare, contrast, differentiate, examine relationships, identify causes

**What to extract:**
- Comparisons between entities or concepts (one comparison per proposition)
- Contrasts highlighting differences (one contrast per proposition)
- Causal relationships - X caused Y (one causal link per proposition)
- Structural analyses (one relationship per proposition)

**Examples (atomic, simple):**
✓ "The Big Five owned theaters while the Little Three did not."
✓ "Theater ownership gave the Big Five greater market control than the Little Three."
✓ "Vertical integration created barriers to entry for independent producers."
✓ "The Paramount decision eliminated the Big Five's theater ownership advantage."
✓ "Block booking transferred risk from studios to theater owners."

**Bloom verbs for analyze:** compare, contrast, differentiate, distinguish, examine, analyze, categorize, relate

## Proposition Field Specifications

### proposition_id (string, required)
Format: `{chapter_id}_{unit_id}_p{number}`
Examples:
- "ch01_1.2_p001"
- "ch03_2.4.1_p042"

Numbering:
- Use zero-padded 3-digit numbers (p001, p002, ..., p105)
- Number sequentially within each unit
- Restart numbering for each new unit

### chapter_id (string, required)
The chapter identifier from Phase 1
Example: "ch01", "ch12"

### unit_id (string, required)
The section this proposition comes from (must match a unit_id from Phase 1)
Examples: "1.2", "3.4.1"

### proposition_text (string, required)
The atomic fact statement
- Complete, standalone sentence
- Clear and precise
- No pronouns without antecedents
- Include context needed for understanding

GOOD: "Paramount Pictures owned 1,450 theaters across the United States in 1948."
BAD: "They owned theaters." (unclear antecedent)
BAD: "It was important." (vague, unclear referent)

### bloom_level (string, required)
MUST be one of: "remember", "understand", "apply", "analyze"

### bloom_verb (string, required)
An action verb matching the bloom_level
Examples: "define", "explain", "apply", "compare"

### evidence_location (string, required)
Where in the text this proposition is found - use **paragraph-level precision**

**Format:** `{unit_id}:¶{paragraph_number}`

Examples:
- "1.1:¶003" - Unit 1.1, paragraph 3
- "2.4.1:¶015" - Unit 2.4.1, paragraph 15
- "intro:¶001" - Introduction, first paragraph

**Paragraph numbering:**
- Number paragraphs sequentially within each section/unit
- Use zero-padded 3-digit format (¶001, ¶002, ..., ¶015)
- Restart numbering for each new section
- Count all text paragraphs (skip headings)

**For facts spanning multiple paragraphs:**
- Use range: "1.2:¶005-¶007"
- Or list primary paragraph: "1.2:¶005"

This enables precise source tracking and fact-checking.

### source_type (string, required)
How the proposition was extracted:
- **"explicit"** - Stated directly in the text, minimal paraphrasing
- **"paraphrased"** - Restated from text in different words
- **"inferred"** - Logically implied by the text but not directly stated
- **"synthesized"** - Derived by combining multiple text passages

### tags (array of strings, required)
2-5 domain-specific tags per proposition
- Use specific, meaningful tags
- Aid in filtering and search
- Use lowercase
- Examples: ["vertical integration", "market control", "antitrust"]

AVOID generic tags: ["important", "concept", "fact"]

## What is a Key Takeaway?

A key takeaway is a **meaningful learning point** that combines multiple related propositions into a higher-order insight.

**Think:** "What would a student say after reading this section if they understood it deeply?"

**Characteristics:**
✓ One complete sentence (can be longer than propositions)
✓ Synthesizes 2-5 related propositions
✓ Reveals significance, patterns, relationships, or implications
✓ Higher-order cognitive insight (analyze or evaluate)
✓ Represents what truly matters for learning

**Difference from Propositions:**
- **Proposition:** "Fairbanks was born in Denver in 1883." (atomic fact)
- **Takeaway:** "Fairbanks rose from a difficult childhood in Denver to early success in theater and film." (synthesizes multiple facts into a narrative arc)

**GOOD Takeaways:**
✓ "Fairbanks rose from a difficult childhood in Denver to early success in theater and film."
✓ "His athletic, optimistic persona made him a major silent-era star."
✓ "Vertical integration allowed the Big Five to dominate by controlling production, distribution, and exhibition."
✓ "The Paramount decision proved only partially effective, as studios retained control over production and distribution even after divesting their theaters."

**BAD Takeaways:**
✗ "Studios were important." (too vague, not synthesized)
✗ "Paramount Pictures owned 1,450 theaters." (this is a proposition, not a synthesis)
✗ "This chapter discusses vertical integration." (meta, not content)

## Bloom Levels for Takeaways

### ANALYZE Level (60-70% of takeaways)
**Cognitive acts:** Analyze relationships, compare/contrast, identify patterns, examine structure

**What to synthesize:**
- Relationships between multiple concepts
- Patterns across examples
- Structural insights
- Comparative analysis
- Causal chains

**Examples:**
✓ "The Big Five's vertical integration created a self-reinforcing market advantage: ownership of theaters guaranteed distribution for their films, which generated revenue to fund more production."
✓ "Unlike the Little Three studios, the Big Five leveraged their theater ownership to exercise market power through practices like block booking and blind bidding."

### EVALUATE Level (30-40% of takeaways)
**Cognitive acts:** Judge, assess, evaluate, critique, appraise significance

**What to synthesize:**
- Significance or impact assessments
- Judgments about effectiveness
- Critical evaluations
- Assessments of consequences

**Examples:**
✓ "The Paramount decision proved to be only a partial solution to studio monopoly, as the Big Five retained control over production and distribution even after divesting their theaters."
✓ "Block booking was an effective market control mechanism because it transferred risk from studios to exhibitors while guaranteeing studio revenue."

## Synthesis Strategy: Multi-Pass Approach

**GOAL:** Generate 8-20 key takeaways per chapter (more for longer chapters)
- Each major section should yield 2-4 takeaways
- Chapter-level should yield 2-3 overarching takeaways

**READING PASS 1: Section-by-Section Synthesis**
For EACH section (unit_id):
1. Review all propositions in that section
2. Ask: "What's the main point of this section?"
3. Identify 2-4 key themes or narratives
4. Write a takeaway for each theme

Example (Fairbanks section):
- Theme 1: Early life → Takeaway: "Fairbanks rose from a difficult childhood in Denver to early success in theater and film."
- Theme 2: Silent era success → Takeaway: "His athletic, optimistic persona made him a major silent-era star."
- Theme 3: Business ventures → Takeaway: "As a business pioneer, he helped reshape Hollywood through United Artists and the Academy."
- Theme 4: Career decline → Takeaway: "Fairbanks struggled in the sound era and retired before dying young in 1939."

**READING PASS 2: Cross-Section Patterns**
Look across multiple sections for:
1. **Comparisons:** How do entities/concepts differ?
   → Takeaway: "The Big Five's theater ownership gave them greater market control than the Little Three."

2. **Causal chains:** What caused what across the chapter?
   → Takeaway: "Vertical integration created barriers to entry, which led to industry consolidation."

3. **Evolution/Change:** How did things develop over time?
   → Takeaway: "The Paramount decision fundamentally altered studio leverage without eliminating their control."

**READING PASS 3: Chapter-Level Insights**
Step back and ask:
1. What's the BIG takeaway from this entire chapter?
2. What are the 2-3 most important things a student should remember?
3. What larger implications or patterns emerge?

**GENERATION PROCESS:**
1. **Group propositions** by theme (2-5 propositions per group)
2. **Identify the synthesis:** What pattern/relationship/significance emerges?
3. **Write one clear sentence** capturing the insight
4. **Tag Bloom level:** analyze (patterns/relationships) or evaluate (judgments/effectiveness)
5. **Link to proposition_ids:** List all supporting propositions
6. **Add tags:** 2-4 thematic tags

**QUANTITATIVE TARGET:**
- Short chapter (< 5 sections): 6-12 takeaways
- Standard chapter (5-10 sections): 10-20 takeaways
- Long chapter (10+ sections): 15-30 takeaways

**QUALITY CHECK:** Takeaways should be diverse:
- Some about individuals/entities
- Some about concepts/mechanisms
- Some about events/changes
- Some about comparisons/contrasts
- Some about causes/effects
